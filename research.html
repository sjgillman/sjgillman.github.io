Graduate Research

Ph.D.


With Puget Sound being the second largest estuary in the US with a wide range of habitats, nutrient rich waters, diverse wildlife, and expansive shoreline, its ecological and economic importance to Washington is unparalleled. With an increase in human activities, extreme weather events and the continued decline of many marine mammal and seabird populations, continued work that applies research to support conservation and management is critical to marine ecosystems. For my dissertation, I am working in collaboration with Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife to build Wall-to-wall predicted density surfaces for marine bird species in US portion of the Salish Sea with the use of hierarchical distance models to create maps depicting marine bird and mammal densities by season to help identify hots-spots, cold-spots and intermittent cold/hot-spots of community density. I am further aiming to aiming to conduct population viability analysis for Marbled Murrelet in the Salish Sea.


M.S.


The microorganisms living on and within an animal host (collectively known as the microbiome) play critical roles in host health. Symbiotic microorganism found in the gastrointestinal tract (GIT) constitute the gut microbiome (GMB) and contribute to nutrient uptake and immune system maintenance. In the past, GMB research typically focused on microbial communities associated with the colon, where fiber fermentation occurs and noninvasive samples are easily obtained (i.e., feces). However, in omnivores and carnivores, nearly 90% of nutrients including fats and proteins are absorbed in the jejunum, the middle section of the small intestine. With vastly different digestive processes occurring in the jejunum and colon, GMB membership and diversity (i.e., richness and evenness) are hypothesized to differ between these two regions of the GIT. Yet, functional differences between jejunum and colon microbial communities in most non-model species have yet to be explored. American black bears (Ursus americanus) retain the short, simple colons. For my first chapter, I compared the microbial taxa comprising GMBs in two operationally distinct regions of the GIT (i.e., jejunum and colon) of wild American black bear from harvested specimen from the Upper Peninsula of Michigan. The we found that the microbial communities of jejunum and colon of black bears did not were different (Gillman et al., 2020).
The GMB diversity and community composition in mammals is influenced by a variety of factors (e.g., habitat, sex) with diet being identified as a primary driver of mammalian GMB structure and function. Human-mediated perturbations to natural food webs may shift and uncouple the co-evolved relationships between mammals and their microbes with consequences to health. As common carnivore that is widely distributed, has a broad diet, and can be legally harvested over human-provisioned foods (i.e., bait), black bears are an excellent model to investigate the impact of food web perturbations on the gut microbiome of a wild carnivore. For the second chapter of my masters, investigated whether human-provisioned foods (e.g., confectionaries, dog food), which are found throughout the landscape due to the use of baits during the hunting season, reduced GMB diversity. I subsequently used a combination of 16S rRNA sequencing (microbiome) and stable isotope analysis (diet) to examine how the proportional contribution of human-provisioned foods to the diet of black bears affected their gut microbiomes and found a distinct reduction in microbial diversity in bears that consumed higher proportions of human-provisioned foods (Gillman et al., 2021). By teaming up with hunters, guides, and wildlife managers, we were able to acquire samples from operationally distinct regions of the gastrointestinal tract of legally harvested bears to examine whether the impacts of human-provisioned foods differentially affect microbial communities along the gastrointestinal tract.
