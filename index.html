I’m Sierra Gillman, a Ph.D. Candidate in the School of Environmental and Forest Sciences at the University of Washington, where I work in the Quantitative Ecology Lab. My research focuses on understanding wildlife responses to global environmental change using long-term monitoring, population dynamics, and spatial modeling. Currently, I’m collaborating with the Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife to create density surface models for marine bird species in the Salish Sea, with the goal of supporting conservation and management efforts.

My academic journey has been shaped by diverse research experiences. During my master’s at Northern Michigan University in the WECOS Lab, I explored the gut microbiome of American black bears, investigating the impacts of anthropogenic pressures on microbial diversity. Before that, I earned a B.S. in Biology and Environmental Studies from Florida State University, where my passion for ecological research took root.

Beyond my academic work, I’ve contributed to projects examining the effects of human-provisioned foods on wildlife gut microbiomes, predator-prey dynamics in the Pacific Northwest, and conservation of Madagascar’s unique carnivores and lemurs. As an NSF Graduate Research Fellow and ARCS Scholar, I value the integration of quantitative approaches with applied ecology and am dedicated to mentoring, open science, and collaborative research. When I’m not in the field or at my computer, I enjoy sharing my knowledge through teaching, mentoring, and outreach.
