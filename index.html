About Me

I’m Sierra Gillman, a Ph.D. Student, National Science Foundation (NSF) Graduate Research Fellow, and Achievement Reward for College Scientists (ARCS) Scholar in Dr. Beth Gardner’s Quantitative Ecology Lab located in the School of Environmental and Forest Sciences at the University of Washington. I am interested on working to develop our understanding of wildlife’s responses to global environmental change by studying long-term species monitoring, population dynamics, and distribution patterns using quantitative techniques to model the response of wildlife populations to anthropogenic stressors.
I received my B.S. in Biology and Environmental Studies (double major) from Florida State University in 2013 and my M.S. in Biology in 2020 from Northern Michigan University in Dr. Diana Lafferty’s Wildlife Ecology and Conservation Science Lab where I studied the gut microbiome of wild American black bears. During my M.S., I worked on collaborative projects focused on understanding the potential ramifications of anthropogenic pressures on wildlife gut microbiomes (GMBs), including investigating the impacts of human-provisioned foods on wild black bear GMBs diversity (M.S. chapter), habitat fragmentation on American marten GMBs, and long-term radiation exposure to wild boar GMBs inhabiting the Fukushima Nuclear Exclusion Zone.

Work experience

Starkey Experimental Forest, ODFW, OR

Research Technician Sep & Nov 2017; Apr - Jun'18
At Starkey Experimental Forest, I was a technician for a collaborative project between OSU and ODFW working for a project focused on interactions between four carnivores present in the area (cougars, coyotes, bobcats, and bears), and additionally their influences on prey populations. Part of my role there was deploying and helping to maintain camera trap grids, assisting in captures, and collaring of the four carnivores, and tracking collared individuals.

In this role I collected forestry and vegetation data, including performing dbh, canopy height, boring for age, vegetation collection and identification, built temporary pens for hand raised deer, behavioral observations, while spending 9 days at a time camping.


WildCRU, University of Oxford, Madagascar

Research Technician for DPhil Candidate, May - Aug'15
In the Menebe region of Madagascar, I worked in western site. Dr. Merson’s was investigating the anthropogenic threats facing Madagascar’s largest carnivore, the fosa by examining socio-economic, and geographic. Dr. Merson also studied the effects of habitat disturbance and exotic species presence on the spatio-temporal occurrence of fosa through large-scale camera-trapping in forests. In my role, I help establish and maintain camera trap grids to monitor fosa, assisted in surveying local communities to better understand human-fosa conflict, and scheduled traveling from site to site


Madagascar Biodiversity Partnership, Madagascar

Research Assistant, Prolemur simus, Sep'14 - Mar'15

MBP’s Greater Bamboo lemur team works with the local malagasy community to conduct a comprehensive study on the population genetics and behavior of the greater bamboo lemur inorder to develop an effective in situ conservation plans. For this project, MBP implemented non-invasive techniques that rely on fecal samples as source of DNA. During my role as a field technician, I spent 5 days a week conducting focal studies to monitor behavior, activity, and diet of the lemurs in the area. I also collected fecal samples for DNA analysis, worked with local guides, and ran the day-to-day logistics on site for the project.







Wildlife Safari Park, OR

Carnivore Keeper, May'13 - Sep'14
As a carnivore keeper for WSP’s cheetah breeding program, which is part of the Association of Zoo and Aquarium’s Species Survival Plan, I focused not only on the care of cheetahs and other large carnivores, but also studied cheetah breeding behavior, participated in artificial insemination projects, raised and monitored abandon cubs, and conducted outreach across the Pacific Northwest with ambassador cheetahs. It was here while monitoring the breeding behavior that I realized I wanted to continue down the path of wildlife research and ultimately lead me to where I am today.
