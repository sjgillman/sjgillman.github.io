About Me

and Achievement Reward for College Scientists (ARCS) Scholar in the Quantitative Ecology Lab located
in the School of Environmental and Forest Sciences at the University of Washington.

Although I was born in South Florida, in my preteens I moved to a small farm in rural Tennessee where I
spent most of my time exploring the outdoors. At an early age I became fascinated by the behavior of the
wildlife that surrounded me. By the time I was twelve I had already made my first attempt at 'field research',
monitoring the nesting sites and ranges of the Eastern box turtles that lived in the woods around me. Despite my
rudimentary approach, by collecting individuals and noting their shell markings, releasing and 'tracking' the turtles,
I was able to gain an insight into their life history. The sense of excitement and achievement I gleaned from this nurtured
my already keen interest in the natural world and was undoubtedly instrumental in the development of my passion and
desire to pursue a career aimed at better understanding and protecting the animals I have grown to admire and love.
I received my B.S. in Biology and Environmental Studies (double major) from Florida State University in 2013 and
my M.S. in Biology in 2020 from Northern Michigan University in the Wildlife Ecology and Conservation Science
Lab where I studied the gut microbiome of wild American black bears (Ursus americanus). During my M.S.,
I worked on collaborative projects focused on understanding the potential ramifications of anthropogenic pressures
on wildlife gut microbiomes (GMBs), including investigating the impacts of human-provisioned foods on wild black bear
GMBs diversity (M.S. chapter), habitat fragmentation on American marten (Martes americana) GMBs, and long-term radiation
